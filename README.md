# Epidemiological Modeling of SARS-CoV-2

This project explores the mathematical and computational modeling of infectious disease transmission through the implementation and analysis of classical epidemiological frameworks, including the SI (Susceptible-Infected), SIS (Susceptible-Infected-Susceptible), and SIR (Susceptible-Infected-Removed) models. The objective is to investigate epidemic dynamics, understand the mechanisms governing disease spread, and evaluate how different epidemiological parameters influence infection trends over time.

The study combines theoretical epidemiology, differential equation modeling, numerical simulation, and data-driven validation. Starting from fundamental compartmental models, the project progressively introduces more realistic assumptions such as recovery, immunity, and population transitions between epidemiological states. Particular attention is devoted to the SIR framework, which is widely used in public health research to describe the evolution of infectious diseases.

A Python implementation is provided to simulate epidemic outbreaks under different transmission and recovery scenarios. The simulations allow the analysis of key epidemiological phenomena, including exponential growth, infection peaks, disease persistence, and epidemic decline. Model behavior is examined by varying parameters such as the transmission rate (λ), recovery/removal rate (γ), and initial population conditions.

To assess the practical relevance of the models, the simulated epidemic curves are compared with real-world COVID-19 observations. This validation process highlights both the strengths and limitations of compartmental epidemiological models when applied to complex real populations.
